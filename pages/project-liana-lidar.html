Liana Infestation in
Sal Forests

Funder: Research Management Wing, GAU  |  Status: Ongoing
Role
Principal Investigator (PI)
Funder
Research Management Wing, Gazipur Agricultural University
Duration
2024 – 2026
Study Area
Sal Forests (Shorea robusta), Bangladesh
Remote Sensing Platform
GEDI (satellite LiDAR) + Sentinel-2 optical imagery
Key Methods
LiDAR canopy profiling, change detection, spatial statistics

Overview

Liana (woody climbing plant) infestation is one of the most pressing ecological threats facing Sal forest ecosystems in Bangladesh. Lianas suppress canopy tree growth, reduce timber quality, and alter light regimes beneath the canopy — ultimately reducing biodiversity and forest carbon stocks.

This project pioneers the use of high-resolution satellite-borne LiDAR (Global Ecosystem Dynamics Investigation — GEDI) combined with multi-temporal Sentinel-2 optical imagery to map, quantify, and model liana infestation trends across Bangladesh's Sal forest landscape.

Research Objectives

The project has three integrated objectives: (1) map the current extent and density of liana infestation using LiDAR vertical canopy structure metrics; (2) analyse temporal trends in infestation between 2019–2024 using multi-date optical satellite imagery; and (3) identify key ecological and anthropogenic drivers of infestation spread through spatial regression modelling.

Methodology

Canopy height models (CHMs) derived from GEDI LiDAR footprints are used to detect liana-induced canopy disruption — characterised by irregular height reduction and increased canopy roughness. These structural indicators are cross-validated with field surveys at permanent sampling plots distributed across the study area.

Spectral indices from Sentinel-2 (NDVI, EVI, red-edge chlorophyll index) provide complementary phenological information, allowing seasonal patterns of infestation to be tracked over time. Machine learning classifiers trained on field-verified ground truth data produce infestation probability maps at 10 m resolution.

Expected Outcomes

This project will produce the first comprehensive, spatially-explicit assessment of liana infestation in Bangladesh's Sal forests using space-borne LiDAR — a methodological advance that can be replicated across other tropical forest systems. Outputs include peer-reviewed publications, policy briefs for the Bangladesh Forest Department, and open-access infestation maps.
